And Rui looks exhausted now. He looks over to Deng as though this time, the hundredth time, she might offer to tell the story as it really goes. She only eyes him coolly over the rim of her mug. Instead, Ma Zhuming picks up the slack.

“I was Xia’s eyes inside the police,” Ma begins. He has a voice built and honed for briefings. “I was his confidant; I thought I was his best friend. He pauses, I think, so that we all feel the tug. The biggest fishbowl I’ve ever seen is just outside. And Deng, Rui, Ma, and me, none of us are all here. We’re all silently itching to go watch it.

“Blue Delta paid attention to the wrong part of the Xia Papers,” he concludes. “They — we — imagined the public horrified by lies about what the computer system was capable of, what it had managed to build beneath the city as its logic unraveled. We staked everything on that. But what the public really cared about was the Ripples. They — we — were fascinated. Smitten. No one should have been surprised when people started putting the cameras back up.”

And thus, the Weather Bureau. Neither Rui or Ma ever own their allegiance to it. They speak about it like a third party, talking circles around how it came to be: from strands of the police force, and Blue Delta’s bureaucracy, and academia. The municpal weather bureau was a convenient cloak: harmless, nonpartisan, a footprint in every ward. As another world bloomed openly on screens across Shanghai, it consumed their expertise in fluid dynamics and predictive modeling. Ripplechasing. The Chalkers. Quadratic belief — or, if you like, mass psychosis, collective delusion. People were hallucinating more in the Sea than the poor weathermen knew what to do with. The thing outgrew its carapice.

Its new shell was YINS.

It was built on top of the old Mirror Sea command center.

“Easier than you’d think,” Rui chuckles. “To weave something like this into a research university. No one really understands what other people are working on. These days, we barely understand our own work.”

This is where I really start to dig in with questions. I ask over and over again what came first. Did the Weather Bureau infiltrate YINS, found YINS, because it had begun to find neikotic eggs in the Sea? Or had that come after, when more of the observation was done from loop-lock? Neither Rui or Ma can really say. Deng takes a smirking pleasure in watching them squirm, her point seemingly made.

“Suowei — I believe, I’d have to check — was the first to find a neikotic egg in the hyperlagmites. It contained nothing. It was like a seashell on the beach. Pick it up, and hear the ocean. But it undeniably had the form. Months later, they found a second one. It had some minor algorithmic utility. Suowei started quietly buying up Mirror Sea feeds. Paracoin and Chaoyue blindly followed, for years, before they caught on to the why.”

Between Suowei and the other fintechs, there are a few dozen people looking for eggs in the Sea. Their people don’t talk each other, but they do talk to the Weather Bureau. They — and YINS — have ways to launder those discoveries as human.
