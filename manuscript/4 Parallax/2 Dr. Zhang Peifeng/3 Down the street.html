The conversation proceeds in this sort of circle, and in the end I finally insist that he just show me. Quietly, against Min’s footsteps and tuneless whistling, he leads me through a defunct emergency exit, putting us on the street outside Old New Prosperity. It’s not the way I came. I trail him for several blocks, two paces behind. Tethi walks weightless, breathless; If I closed my eyes I’d swear I was the only one here. We’re not two blocks from the hatched-out grey area on my map. I can see into it from here, see that it has a beauty all its own.

In the very embers of daylight, it could be a normal Shanghai street: just another mile of mopeds and magnolias. But, okay, that’s a lot of stuff, belonging to everyone and no one. Pushcarts and satellite arrays and blackened-out grow towers. A decommissioned military veetle half-tucked beneath a tarp. A silent chorus of red LEDs blinks from the walls dense with cameras, and from wires stretched taut across the narrow lane. No other light, not really. The streetlights will be emitting that infrared color-of-nothing for the Mirror Sea’s eyes only. Something shifts, and momentarily all I can see is the chalk: ever-present on the walls of Shanghai, but thickening here into a brilliant white-on-black blur, minute detail crammed into the negative space of sigils within sigils within sigils. It looks like the back entrance to another world.

“Don’t go in there,” Tethi tells me flatly.

But then, of all things, he produces a nub of chalk and puts it to the wall of what was once a charge-swap station. “Yes and no,” he mutters, answering the unspoken, obvious question. “There are layers to it. I’ve picked up some tricks.” He pulls a long, left-handed stroke across the brick, slowly at first, like a thief picking for tumblers. It hooks suddenly, and there’s a received, passive quality to the slashes and arcs he draws next. The darkness around me feels formless, liquid. The shape of this street is, surely, interchangeable with another. My head spins with a smearing of position, a sharp wherewards momentum. When he’s finished, he reaches for a door that part of my brain insists wasn’t there before.

Behind it, quasigraphic candles flicker along the an uneven floor, their false angles revealed in the shatterglass and moped mirrors lining the the walls. Thick cables dangle parabolically from the ceiling, emerging from grey PVC pipe stamped at regular intervals with that familiar blind-eye insignia.

“The Mirror Sea,” I state the obvious, starting too loud and falling quiet halfway through that, startled by the echo of my own voice.

“A Mirror Sea,” he corrects. And, sure, he does seem to be one to make such a chiding distinction. The Mirror Sea fell with Xia Zitian; what we have now are fragmented subnets, tidepools and shards. He runs his finger along a cable as it reaches a splice-box. “Three, actually, that have been joined together. A triple point.”

My mind reels, imagining all the places these cables could lead — anywhere in the city, or all the way to Suzhou. Any set of cameras, with their particular placement and angle, together point to a unique part of the Mirror Sea. I saw the view through a single camera earlier  —  not quite nothing. Perhaps only Xia knows what you’d see with all of them.

“It’s getting harder for regular people to access.” The lack of light helps me pick these qualities from his whisper: defensive, prideful, Francophone, and vaguely drunk. “They get what the Weather Bureau will let them see. Which is time-delayed flats and the odd quasigram. But the raw stuff…you need to be someone, or know someone.”

“Or fall in with the Nine-Eyes,” I venture. There’s another way to see the Sea, of course, which is to promise yourself that it’s already there, that it’s always been there. To follow the Chalk down its steepest quadratic valleys, surrendering your agency in whole. He makes himself busy unlocking another door, and so doesn’t respond to this, but I see his eyes flash.

The next room brings sweet, visible, fluorescent light. A UTMS scanner in the back corner, I think — I haven’t seen anything quite this ramshackle since the one Deng hacked together at Stanford. It has all the reassuring qualities of a county fair roller coaster, but at least the EASL arrays look off-the-shelf. Next to the keyboard are several copies of the Sunflower Sieve egg. I offer an appreciative little phew sound. It’s janky and damp, but c’mon. This is all right up my alley.

“Do they have any idea that you’re doing this?”

It’s a stupid question. The Nine-Eyes have been known to smash up unremarkable Mirror Sea displays miles from their redoubts. Watching their own patch of the Sea from loop-lock is a middle finger to their central faith, a turn from the sublime to the pornographic. Tethi laughs humorlessly. “I’ll know when they find out.” He’s flicking more switches now, kicking the EASL array to free it from a bootloop. “Let me ask you. You ever consider why the market rate of a Mirror Sea feed is so high these days? Why the Big Three are snatching them all up?”

I thought I had. Suowei buys feeds because that’s what Suowei does: amasses a dragon’s-lair treasury of assets, homogenizing them into the silken threads from which the parallel yuan is spun. Suowei has teams of astrologers operating lasers in space, melting asteroids that it deems inauspicious. Suowei’s reality-distortion field bulges to expand across the globe, and it’s only here in its dead center that anything they do seems remotely sane, remotely sober. Chaoyue, Paracoin — they just follow Suowei’s lead, trying to keep the triumvirate less isosceles.

No, I haven’t really considered it. I shake my head.

The scanner is purring under my hand now. I’m trying to find comfort in the familiar noises it’s making, but my head is throbbing, my mouth is dry. “Min’s Mix,” Tethi tells me once I’m sitting inside it. “Some 5-subs in here, probably spicier than the YINS breakfast blend. I’d spread out my zeroes to compensate.” He flicks an autovial and slots it into the machine. “I don’t want to bias you, so I’m just going to put you in. Let you see what I saw. Afterward, we can talk about it.” He clicks through popups complaining that his free trial of Kanwei Pharos is years expired. “Afterward, I’m going to ask a favor of you.”

I don’t like that knowing look. He seems to see how badly I want this, and how badly I need to keep that longing under control. I try to imagine I’m Cai, who does this all day, who keeps the queasy quadratic madness always at arm’s length. I channel her airy detachment as I pull the scanner hood over my eyes.

“So put me in.”
